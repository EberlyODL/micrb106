The physical boundary of any cell is the plasma membrane. It encloses the fluid environment, or cytoplasm, found on the inside of all cells. The membrane is made up of a double layer of phospholipids arranged in a certain pattern. Phospholipids have both polar head groups and non-polar tails. This makes the molecule a bit neurotic when it's placed into water. One of the only stable formations is a phospholipid bilayer. When a bilayer is formed, the polar head groups are placed on the outside near water, while the non-polar tails are on the inside.

Proteins

Embedded in the phospholipid bilayer of the plasma membrane are many different kinds of proteins.

These proteins serve a variety of important functions. Two of the most important functions are cell signaling and membrane transport. Cell signaling is important in regulating and controlling the activities of the organ systems of the body. Insulin is a signaling protein that binds to proteins in the membrane of cells and helps to control glucose levels in the blood. When this signaling process is not functioning properly, diabetes results.

The other function of cell membrane proteins involves cell transport. It is vital to regulate the flow of nutrients across the plasma membrane. Cells need to control what enters or leaves the cytoplasm.

 

Selectively Permeable Membrane

Since a large portion of the membrane is made up of the nonpolar regions of phospholipids, many things cannot easily cross from one side to the other. This makes the membrane Selectively Permeable. Anything that is non-polar will have no problem passing through the membrane. Things like alcohol or steriods are non-polar and can freely cross the membrane. However, anything that is particularly large or strongly polar will not be able to pass through the non-polar region of the membrane. Remember, Polar and non-polar compounds do not mix. Things that do not cross the membrane include large molecules like carbohydrates or amino acids or small molecules with charges, like ions.

Water is an exception to these rules. Water is a polar molecule. It is, however, weakly polar and is also relatively small. Therefore water can in fact freely cross the membrane.
