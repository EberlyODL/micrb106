Physical and Chemical Barriers

Non-specific immune defenses include a number of general barriers that prevent invaders from gaining entry into the body. These are considered nonspecific because they do not target any particular invader, rather they keep everything out.

Barrier Function(s)
Skin Forms durable barrier; constant cell shedding removes pathogens; acidic pH repels microorganisms.
Tears Lubricate and wash eyes; contain lysozyme.

Saliva

 Lubricates mouth and rinses particles into digestive tract; contains lysozyme.
Earwax Traps particles and microorganisms.
Digestive Acids Hydrochloric acid kills most pathogens that enter stomach.
Mucus Traps pathogens so they can be swept away by cilia and then removed by swallowing or sneezing.
Urination Slightly acidic; flushes out urinary tract.
Normal Flora Control harmful organisms by competing against them.

Skin 

This is the most important barrier, as it covers all of the internal tissues and keeps bacteria and viruses from gaining entry. The low moisture, mild pH effects, and oily residues all inhibit microbial growth on the surface of the skin. In fact, the places that tend to be moist part of the time, like the feet, are the places where microbes can grow best.

Lysozyme

This antibacterial enzyme is found in tears and saliva. It reduces the chances of an infection in the eyes and help to control the growth of bacteria in the oral cavity.

Normal Flora

Many harmless bacteria live on the skin and in parts of the digestive tract. These harmless bacteria compete for food and space with any pathogens that may be present, thus keeping the more harmful bacteria at low population levels.

Stomach Acid

The acid content of the stomach prevents most intestinal illnesses. Few bacteria can pass through the stomach alive and make it to the small intestine to cause disease.

Urinary Tract

Another access point for invaders is the urinary tract. The low pH and high salt of urine help flush the urinary tract and prevent microbes from moving up into the bladder to cause an infection. Drinking cranberry juice makes urine more acidic and thus more effective at cleansing the tract.

Macrophages

These immune system cells are found in the tissues and they're always screening for invaders. Macrophages continually search for anything foreign and then destroy it by a process called phagocytosis. Phagocytosis is a specialized form of endocytosis where foreign material, like a bacterial cell, is brought into the cell and exposed to degradative enzymes to destroy it.

 

Complement Proteins

These are a group of proteins found in the bloodstream that can attack bacteria if an antibody is attached to it.

Lymphokines

These are chemical signals that include interleukins and interferon. These are signals used by immune system cells to coordinate attacks on foreign invaders.
